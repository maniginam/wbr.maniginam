The West Baton Rouge Museum: Preserving Louisiana's Sugar Heritage

June 3, 2026 History & Culture

On a six-acre campus at 845 North Jefferson Avenue in Port Allen, directly across the Mississippi River from the Louisiana State Capitol, sits the only museum in the country dedicated to tracing the complete history of sugar in Louisiana. The West Baton Rouge Museum tells that story from the colonial period to the present day, and it does so with a depth and honesty that few small-town museums achieve.

How Sugar Shaped a Parish

The story of sugar in South Louisiana begins in 1751, when Jesuit priests planted a few stalks of cane on Baronne Street in New Orleans. Over the next two and a half centuries, sugar transformed the landscape, economy, and culture of the river parishes west of Baton Rouge. Today, Louisiana produces approximately 13 million tons of cane annually across 22 parishes, generating around $3 billion each year.

West Baton Rouge Parish was at the heart of this industry. Plantations lined the Mississippi River, and sugar mills processed the harvest each fall. The Cinclare Sugar Mill in Brusly operated from 1897 until 2005, making it the last sugar mill in the parish. Its closure marked the end of an era that had defined the region for over two centuries.

The Museum's Founding

The West Baton Rouge Historical Association, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit, founded the museum in 1968 with a mission to research, collect, preserve, and present artifacts and documents reflecting the history and cultural heritage of West Baton Rouge Parish. In 2009, the museum earned accreditation from the American Alliance of Museums, a distinction held by fewer than 10% of American museums and a testament to its professional standards.

The 33-Foot Sugar Mill Model

The centerpiece of the museum's gallery is a 33-foot model of a sugar mill, originally constructed for the 1904 Louisiana Purchase Exposition in St. Louis. After the world's fair ended, the model nearly disappeared from public view. It was the Cinclare Sugar Mill that saved it, reassembling the model at the West Baton Rouge Museum in 1995 to commemorate the 200th anniversary of commercial sugar production in Louisiana.

Surrounding the model, the exhibit features sugarcane murals showing visitors the actual height of cane at harvest, along with detailed panels explaining the process of planting, harvesting, and milling. A dedicated section honors Norbert Rillieux, a free man of color born in New Orleans in 1806, whose invention of the multiple-effect evaporator revolutionized sugar processing worldwide. Rillieux's innovation made the process faster, more efficient, and significantly safer for workers.

Historic Structures on Campus

The museum campus preserves several original structures that bring the past into physical form:

  • The Aillet House (circa 1830) is a French Creole home that represents the architectural traditions brought to Louisiana by early settlers. Its raised design and gallery porch are characteristic of the style that adapted European building traditions to the subtropical climate.
  • 1850s Slave Cabins from Allendale Plantation stand as a direct, unvarnished reminder of the human cost of the sugar industry. These structures are among the most important artifacts on the campus, documenting the lives of the enslaved people who built and sustained the plantation economy.
  • The Reed Shotgun House (1938) represents the housing style common among working-class families in the post-slavery era, with its distinctive linear layout where every room connects in a straight line.
  • The Arbroth Plantation Store (1880s) served as a grocery store from the Reconstruction era through the 1980s. Major Martin Glynn owned Arbroth and two other plantations after the Civil War, producing sugar cane and cotton until the 1940s when the family transitioned to cattle ranching.

Innovators Who Changed the Industry

Beyond Rillieux, the museum highlights other innovators whose work transformed sugar production in Louisiana:

Allen Ramsey Wurtele changed how sugar cane was harvested on his Pointe Coupee Parish plantation in the 1930s. His mechanical innovations helped move the industry away from the backbreaking hand labor that had defined harvest season since the colonial period.

Leonard Julien, born in Ascension Parish in 1910, invented a new method of planting sugar cane in the 1960s. These practical innovations, developed by working farmers rather than academics, represent a tradition the museum calls "Rural Engineuity," a term that became the title of a dedicated exhibit exploring how everyday Louisianans solved engineering problems in the field.

Juke Joints and the Culture of Cane

Sugar shaped more than the economy. It shaped the culture. West Baton Rouge was famous for its juke joints, the informal bars and music venues that provided relief for workers coming in from the cane fields and long days of labor on the Mississippi River. Music played all night through open windows across the canebrake, and these gathering places became incubators for blues, zydeco, and the musical traditions that define South Louisiana today.

The museum preserves this cultural history alongside the agricultural and industrial story, recognizing that the full heritage of sugar includes the music, food, and community bonds that grew up around the industry.

Visiting the Museum

The West Baton Rouge Museum is open Tuesday through Saturday from 10:00 AM to 4:30 PM and Sunday from 2:00 PM to 5:00 PM. It is closed on Mondays. Admission is free, and walk-in tours are personalized to visitors' interests, generally running between one and two hours.

The museum hosts regular events, exhibit openings, and educational programs throughout the year. For current programming, contact the museum at (225) 336-2422 or visit westbatonrougemuseum.com.

Whether you are a lifelong WBR resident who has never stepped inside or a newcomer trying to understand what makes this parish tick, the West Baton Rouge Museum is the single best place to start. The story of sugar is the story of this place, and the museum tells it without flinching.
