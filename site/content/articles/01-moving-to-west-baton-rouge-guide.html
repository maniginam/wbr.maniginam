Moving to West Baton Rouge: A Complete Guide for Families

June 3, 2026 Community Guide

West Baton Rouge Parish sits just across the Mississippi River from Louisiana's capital city, offering families a quieter, more affordable alternative to East Baton Rouge while keeping downtown Baton Rouge within a short commute. With a population of roughly 28,500 residents spread across Port Allen, Brusly, and Addis, WBR delivers small-town community feel with big-city access.

The Three Towns of West Baton Rouge

Port Allen is the parish seat and the largest community. It sits directly across the Horace Wilkinson Bridge (I-10) from downtown Baton Rouge. Port Allen has the most commercial development in the parish, with restaurants, shops, and services along LA Highway 1 and Court Street. It is also home to the West Baton Rouge Museum and the parish government offices.

Brusly lies just south of Port Allen and is known for its highly rated schools and family-oriented neighborhoods. Many families specifically target Brusly for the school district, which consistently outperforms parish and state averages. The town has a quiet, residential character with newer subdivisions mixed among older, established streets.

Addis is the southernmost community, situated along I-10 near the Atchafalaya Basin. It tends to offer the most affordable housing in the parish and has seen steady growth in recent years with new commercial development along LA Highway 1.

Schools: The Brusly Advantage

West Baton Rouge Parish Public Schools serves about 4,378 students across 10 schools with a student-teacher ratio of 14 to 1. The district earned a District Performance Score of 86.7, and the graduation rate has climbed from 83% to 89% over the past five years.

The standout schools are concentrated in the Brusly feeder pattern. Lukeville Elementary School ranks in the top 12% of Louisiana elementary schools. Brusly Middle School and Brusly High School consistently rank among the top public schools in the state, with Brusly High earning a B+ from Niche and maintaining a 13:1 student-teacher ratio with 734 students.

The Port Allen schools serve a different demographic and have lower proficiency rates, though they benefit from smaller class sizes (12:1 student-teacher ratio at Port Allen High). Port Allen High enrolls about 457 students in grades 9-12.

There are no private schools within the parish, but several private and parochial options in Baton Rouge are accessible across the bridge, including Episcopal High School, Catholic High, and St. Joseph's Academy.

Cost of Living and Housing

WBR offers significantly more house for the money compared to popular East Baton Rouge neighborhoods. The median home sale price in West Baton Rouge Parish is approximately $244,000, with a median price per square foot of $151. Homes typically sell within 47 days of listing.

The median household income in the parish is $87,320, which is well above the Louisiana state median. Property taxes are generally lower than East Baton Rouge, though exact rates depend on your specific location and any special taxing districts.

New construction in Brusly subdivisions tends to run higher, often $280,000 to $400,000 for a three- to four-bedroom home. Older homes in Port Allen and properties in Addis can be found well below the parish median.

Flood Zones: Know Before You Buy

Flood risk is a reality across south Louisiana, and WBR is no exception. Approximately 44% of properties in the parish face some level of flood risk over the next 30 years. FEMA classifies areas into zones ranging from low-risk (Zone X) to high-risk (Zone AE), and your zone determines whether flood insurance is mandatory with a mortgage.

Key things to know before buying in WBR:

  • Get an elevation certificate. This determines your actual risk and insurance premium, regardless of what FEMA maps say.
  • Budget for flood insurance. Premiums range from $400-500 per year in Zone X to $1,000-3,000 or more in Zone AE under FEMA's Risk Rating 2.0.
  • Do not rely on Zillow or Realtor.com for flood zone data. Always verify through the parish website or FEMA's Flood Map Service Center.
  • Check the West Baton Rouge Parish flood zone information available through wbrparish.org for official data.

Properties outside designated flood zones in the greater Baton Rouge area have appreciated about 8% faster than those inside flood zones between 2020 and 2024.

The Commute: Living with the Bridge

The single biggest factor in daily life for WBR residents who work in Baton Rouge is the Horace Wilkinson Bridge on I-10. This bridge carries roughly 60,000 vehicles per day, and eastbound traffic narrows from three lanes to one through lane, creating daily bottlenecks.

Morning rush typically peaks between 7:00 and 8:30 AM. Average commute times run about 27 minutes one-way, but incidents on the bridge can cause gridlock that ripples through the entire freeway system. Experienced commuters check traffic cameras daily before leaving home.

The good news: DOTD is widening I-10 by adding one travel lane in each direction from LA 415 to the I-10/I-12 split, which should improve capacity. There is also the "old bridge" (US 190) farther north, which can serve as an alternate route depending on where you live and work.

The best advice from locals: if you can, live on the same side of the river as your workplace. If your job is in Baton Rouge, factor the bridge commute into your quality-of-life calculation honestly.

What Makes WBR Worth It

Despite the bridge, families keep choosing West Baton Rouge for good reasons. The Brusly schools rival the best in the metro area. Housing costs give you more space and land than comparable East Baton Rouge neighborhoods. The parish has a genuine small-town feel where neighbors know each other, youth sports leagues are a community event, and Friday night football at Brusly High is a local institution.

The West Baton Rouge Museum, free public parks with splash pads and walking tracks, the community pool at Williams and Lee, and easy access to the Atchafalaya Basin for fishing and outdoor recreation round out a lifestyle that many families find hard to beat at this price point.

For families weighing the move, the formula is straightforward: visit on a weekday morning, drive the bridge at rush hour, tour the schools, and talk to people at Court Street Cafe or Cou-Yon's. West Baton Rouge sells itself to the right family.
